# Dungeoneering

At the core of D12 Expeditions is the dungeon. Any location can be a dungeon as long as it has the following components:
- Entrances
- Barriers that enclose discreet areas
- Enclosed sections that form an individual Set Piece.


# Point Crawl
When traveling through known or well charted territory the party moves from point to point over a leg of their journey. This could be a single starting point and an end point, or it could be a series of points between the start and end points. It allows the Game Master flexibility in how many encounters the party has over the journey, how many possibilities for getting lost they want to include, and how many choices they want the party to make in their journey.
## Features of a Point Crawl
There are two main features beyond the start and end of the journey, legs of the journey, the points of choice, and way points. 
### Legs
The time between points are the Legs of the journey where Encounters happen.

> ###### Navigation Roll
> Navigation between points takes a Complex Roll of an Aptitude + Knowledge to chart the path; Wits + Senses to be aware of threats on the road; and Fortitude + Athletics to keep a pace that all party members can maintain. The Target Number should be based on the familiarity and how clearly marked the path is. As with all Complex Rolls, there should be a Perk that either speeds up their journey or provides the party with a beneficial encounter or both before they reach the next Point. As a Success they should reach the next Point when expected. As a Complication they have an Encounter with a Conflict. And on a Failure they should get lost, requiring spending additional time to find their location and make a new Navigation Roll to get them back on track, possibly with an Encounter.

# Hex Crawl
When exploring new territory with little to information or searching for a location the Hex Crawl is the best tool for the job. There are two ways to generate the map, either the Game Master builds the map before the game begins and reveals the hexes as the party moves through them or the map is generated as the characters explore.

## Navigation
Moving through hexes requires a Complex Roll -- its Target Number depending on the type of terrain -- of; Wits + Senses to spot paths and openings in the terrain; Aptitude + Senses to know where you are in the hex and predict the terrain; and Fortitude + Athletics to keep a pace that all party members can maintain. On a Success they move through the Hex and the Game Master rolls the Hex's Random Encounter. As a Perk the Game Master has a -4 on the roll. As a Complication the Game Master has a +4 on the roll. On a Failure roll the Random encounter with a +6 then roll on the Lost table.

## Familiarity
As the party's understanding of a hex improves their Familiarity increases from Unknown, to Explored, then to Charted.
###### Unknown
The party is entering the Hex for the first time. Traversing an Unknown Hex takes three hours and a TN 10 Navigation Complex Roll.
###### Explored
The party has passed through the Hex previously and is familiar with the terrain. Traversing an Explored Hex takes two hours and a TN 6 Navigation Complex Roll.
###### Charted
The party has spent time charting the Hex previously and has deep understanding of the terrain. Traversing a Charted Hex takes 1 hour and a TN 4 Navigation Complex Roll. 
## Traversing
The nature of the terrain will modify the time it takes to traverse and the Target Number for the Navigation Complex Roll. 

| Terrain          | Time Modifier | TN  |
| :--------------- | :-----------: | :-: |
| Open plain       |      +0       | -2  |
| Lightly Forested |      +1       | -0  |
| Heavily Forested |      +2       | +1  |
| Hills            |      x2       | +2  |
| Mountains        |      x4       | +3  |
## Random Encounters
When rolling a Random Encounter rolling twice and combining the outputs will produce more dynamic encounters than just rolling once. Depending on the relative danger or safety of the terrain either adds or subtracts a modifier from the roll.

## Set Pieces
Each conflict occurs in a place beyond the environment of the Hex. Abandoned farmhouses, collapsed towers, witches huts, monoliths, and other evocative settings all make a great set pieces. Each set piece should include one or more Terrain Features that the players can interact with or otherwise effect the battlefield.
